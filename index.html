Three layers, drawn honestly before the AI ships.

Every product like yours sits on three stacks at once. The teams that ship the AI layer cleanly are the teams that know which work belongs where. Here is how we would draw it for happyhoa.ai.

60%
Traditional code + database

The mechanical layer.

Ingest the building's documents. Expose them at the URL Florida SB 154 expects. Show a balance. Post a notice. This is Postgres and a clean API, not an LLM.

30%
Rule-based logic

The compliance layer.

Reserve-study triggers. Late-fee schedules. Voting quorums. Pre-packaged Florida templates for SB 4-D, SB 154, 718 and the 2025 disclosure law. Boring work. Wins deals.

10%
Genuine AI

The resident-support layer.

A grounded assistant that answers "what time does the pool close" from the pool rules, and "what section covers short-term rentals" from the bylaws. In Spanish or English. Built on the building's own documents.

The honest part.

You run 6,000+ communities across 10 countries and $40M a month flows through the platform. The US push is real. The booth was at SS88 last year and it is at SS88 again this year, which is a signal. The Florida play is the right play: new state disclosure laws for HOAs of 100+ parcels, a bilingual buyer base in Miami-Dade and Broward, and a national competitor (PayHOA) that just raised $27.5M and is still not mobile-first the way you are.

Here is the piece worth talking about. The eMerge booth copy says HappyHOA "leverages modern technology like AI to improve efficiency and transparency." The sub-brand at happyhoa.ai is referenced in your own materials. The subdomain returns 404 today. That is the tension every scale-up lives with. The marketing has to run ahead of the product, and the product team has to decide which promises are real LLM work, which are rule-based compliance templates, and which are plain code on top of the payments rail you already have.

We think about every product like this in three layers. Roughly 60% of the problem is traditional code and database work (ingesting documents, exposing them at the right URL for Florida SB 154, showing a balance). Roughly 30% is rule-based logic (compliance templates, reserve-study triggers, late-fee schedules, voting quorum rules). Roughly 10% is genuine AI (a resident-support assistant grounded in the building's own documents, in Spanish or English, that knows the difference between "what time does the pool close" and "what section of the bylaws covers short-term rentals"). Teams that skip this split put an LLM where Postgres would do the job faster, cheaper, and with better guarantees. Teams that get this split right ship the AI layer without breaking the rest.

The case that maps closest is a 46-property short-term rental operator we worked with called Simple Lyfe Stays. Multi-unit, ops-heavy, sitting on a large operational document corpus, needing orchestration rather than a from-scratch ML build. We ran a workshop, drew the 60/30/10 split for their stack, and turned the founder's head into a playbook his team could run. Scoped sprint, methodology transfer, playbook delivery. The work compressed cross-property ops from a founder bottleneck to a shared operating system.

The methodology we would bring to the happyhoa.ai rollout is published work. It is called the Interpretable Context Methodology, out through ACM TiiS, with the open repo at github.com/RinDig/Interpretable-Context-Methodology-ICM-. It is the argument for organizing the agent's context (bylaws, resolutions, minutes, budgets) as a layered filesystem so the AI knows what it knows and does not hallucinate answers about the pool hours. That matters for HOAs because trust is the whole business.
